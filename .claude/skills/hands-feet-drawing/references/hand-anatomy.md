## 1. Osteology

### Carpals (8 bones, two rows)

**Proximal row** (wrist to forearm):
- **Scaphoid** — the largest proximal carpal; palpable in the anatomical snuffbox
- **Lunate** — crescent-shaped; articulates with the radius
- **Triquetrum** — pyramidal; on the ulnar side
- **Pisiform** — pea-shaped; sits on top of the triquetrum; palpable as a small bump on the ulnar palm

**Distal row** (toward the fingers):
- **Trapezium** — articulates with the 1st metacarpal (thumb); saddle joint
- **Trapezoid** — smallest carpal; between trapezium and capitate
- **Capitate** — largest carpal; sits at the center of the wrist; head articulates with the lunate
- **Hamate** — hook-shaped; on the ulnar side of the distal row; its hook (hamulus) is palpable on the palm

**Drawing note:** The carpals form a compact mass — do NOT draw individual carpal bones in an artistic rendering. Treat the carpal block as a single rounded mass bridging the forearm to the metacarpals.

### Metacarpals (5 bones)

- Numbered 1 (thumb) to 5 (pinky)
- **3rd metacarpal** is the longest; its head forms the most prominent knuckle (middle finger)
- **1st metacarpal** is the shortest and thickest; it is rotated ~90° relative to the others
- The metacarpal heads (MCP joints) form the visible knuckles when the hand is in a fist
- The metacarpal bases articulate with the distal carpal row

**Drawing note:** The metacarpals fan out from the carpal block. The 2nd and 3rd metacarpals are relatively fixed; the 4th and 5th have more mobility (this is why the pinky side of the hand can "cup" more).

### Phalanges (14 bones)

| Finger | Proximal | Middle | Distal |
|--------|----------|--------|--------|
| Thumb (1) | Yes | No | Yes |
| Index (2) | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Middle (3) | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Ring (4) | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Pinky (5) | Yes | Yes | Yes |

- The distal phalanx of each finger supports the fingernail bed
- The proximal phalanx is the longest segment in each finger
- The middle phalanx is roughly 85% of the proximal phalanx length
- The distal phalanx is roughly 67% of the proximal phalanx length

## 2. Joint Mechanics

| Joint | Type | Motion | Range |
|-------|------|--------|-------|
| Thumb CMC | Saddle | Opposition, flexion/extension, abduction/adduction | High mobility |
| Finger CMC | Plane | Limited gliding | ~10-20° |
| MCP (all) | Condyloid | Flexion/extension, abduction/adduction | ~90° flex, ~20° abd |
| PIP (fingers 2-5) | Hinge | Flexion/extension only | ~100° flex |
| DIP (fingers 2-5) | Hinge | Flexion/extension only | ~80° flex |
| Thumb IP | Hinge | Flexion/extension only | ~80° flex |

**Critical constraints for drawing:**
- PIP and DIP joints can ONLY flex in one plane — no lateral bending. A finger bending sideways at the middle joint is anatomically impossible.
- The MCP joint is the only joint in each finger that can spread (abduct/adduct).
- The thumb CMC joint is the most mobile joint in the hand — it allows the thumb to reach across the palm.

**Knuckle arc:**

When looking at the back of a relaxed open hand, the MCP joints form a subtle curve. The 3rd metacarpal head (middle finger) is the most prominent — it projects the farthest dorsally. The 2nd (index) and 4th (ring) are slightly recessed, and the 5th (pinky) is the least prominent.

When forming a fist, this arc becomes more pronounced. The 3rd MCP is clearly the highest point.

## 3. Soft Tissue & Surface Landmarks

### Dorsal (Back of Hand)

- **Extensor tendons:** Visible as ridges under the skin when fingers are extended. They run from the wrist to the DIP joints of each finger. The index and pinky have additional extensor tendons (extensor indicis, extensor digiti minimi) that create more visible tendon definition.
- **Knuckles:** The MCP heads are visible as rounded prominences. In a fist, the PIP joints also become visible as the second row of knuckles.
- **Veins:** The dorsal venous network is often visible, especially in adults. Veins run between the metacarpal spaces.
- **Nail beds:** Visible on the distal phalanx dorsum.

### Palmar (Palm)

- **Thenar eminence:** The thick, fleshy mound at the base of the thumb. It contains the thenar muscles (abductor pollicis brevis, flexor pollicis brevis, opponens pollicis). This is the most prominent soft tissue mass of the palm.
- **Hypothenar eminence:** The fleshy mound at the base of the pinky. Less prominent than the thenar eminence.
- **Palmar creases (3 major):**
  1. **Distal transverse crease** — runs from the web between index/middle across to the ulnar side. Lines up roughly with the MCP joints of fingers 2-5.
  2. **Proximal transverse crease** — runs from the thumb web across to the ulnar side. Follows the metacarpal heads' curve.
  3. **Thenar crease** — curves around the thenar eminence, separating it from the palm.
- **Pulp pads:** The fingertip pads (pulp) are rounded, fleshy masses on the volar surface of each distal phalanx.

### Interdigital Webbing

The skin folds between fingers reach different distances from the MCP joints:
- Index-middle web: deepest, reaches closest to the PIP joints
- Middle-ring web: slightly shallower
- Ring-pinky web: shallowest

In a relaxed open hand, the webs form gentle curves, not sharp V-shapes.

### Fingernails

- The nail plate sits on the dorsal surface of the distal phalanx
- The nail follows the shape of the underlying bone
- Nails curve in both longitudinal (from base to tip) and transverse (side to side) planes
- The lunula (white crescent at the nail base) is most visible on the thumb and index finger

## 4. Measurement Landmarks

| Measurement | Approximate Value |
|-------------|------------------|
| Hand length (wrist crease to middle fingertip) | ~1 face height |
| Palm length (wrist crease to MCP of middle finger) | ~1/2 hand length |
| Palm width (across MCP joints) | ~2/3 palm length |
| Middle finger length (MCP to tip) | ~1/2 hand length |
| Index finger | ~95% of middle finger length |
| Ring finger | ~93% of middle finger length |
| Pinky finger | ~75% of middle finger length |
| Thumb length (CMC to tip) | ~mid-index proximal phalanx |

## 5. Age & Sex Differences

### Child (under 12)

- Shorter fingers relative to palm size
- More subcutaneous fat — joints are less defined, knuckles are soft
- Fingertip pads are rounder and fuller
- The hand is proportionally larger relative to body size than in adults (hands grow earlier)
- Creases are deeper relative to the small surface area

### Adolescent (12-18)

- Hands and feet often appear disproportionately large — they reach adult size before the rest of the body
- Joint definition becomes more visible
- Veins may become more prominent (particularly in thin individuals)

### Adult Male

- Broader palm, thicker fingers
- Prominent knuckles and joint definition
- More visible veins and extensor tendons on the dorsum
- Larger finger joints
- The hand appears more angular overall

### Adult Female

- Narrower palm, more tapered fingers
- Softer joint definition (more subcutaneous fat)
- Fingernails often longer (cultural)
- The hand appears more curved and streamlined

### Elderly

- Thinner subcutaneous fat — bones and joints are more prominent
- Joint enlargement (Heberden's nodes at DIP, Bouchard's nodes at PIP)
- More prominent veins
- Skin loses elasticity — visible wrinkling and sagging
- Nail plates may become thicker and more curved
- Reduced range of motion (stiffness in joints)
